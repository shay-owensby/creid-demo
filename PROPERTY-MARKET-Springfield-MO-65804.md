## 1. Price Analysis

### Price Trends
- **ZIP 65804 median sale price: ~$296,000 (January 2026), up 7.7% YoY** — outperforming the broader metro. RealtyTrac places the ZIP's median sales price at ~$289,500.
- Springfield citywide figures vary by source: Redfin showed a median of ~$213K (down 1.7% YoY as of late 2025), while Zillow's average home value is $224,619 (up 3.2% YoY). Houzeo reports ~$199,900 (roughly flat).
- Greater Springfield (Greene, Christian, Webster counties) February 2026: median $271,000 (+0.9% YoY), average $316,768 (+4.1% YoY). Greene County average sale price jumped 10.1% YoY to $302,337.
- Local forecasts project a modest **2–4% price increase in 2026**, supported by limited inventory and steady demand.

### Price per Square Foot
- Citywide median: **$135/SF, up 1.5% YoY**. ZIP 65804 commands a premium given its larger homes and southeast-side location near top retail/medical corridors.

### Affordability
- Springfield's median sale price is roughly **43% below the national average** — a key affordability draw.
- However, the city's median household income is only ~$46,000 (≈30% below national), putting the citywide price-to-income ratio near 4.6x. ZIP 65804 skews higher-income, which supports its price premium.
- Affordability remains better than most US metros, but income constraints cap how fast prices can run.

## 2. Supply & Demand

### Inventory Analysis
- **Months of supply: ~2.4** — well below the 6-month balanced threshold; Springfield remains a seller's market in 2026, though less extreme than 2021–2022.
- Inventory is expected to stay tight (2–3 months of supply) through 2026.
- FRED/Realtor.com series for the Springfield CBSA confirm constrained active listing counts relative to historical norms.

### Sales Activity
- Sales volume is **rising**: 470 homes sold in Greater Springfield in February 2026 (+10.8% YoY); Greene County +18.2%.
- But **buyers are slowing down**: average DOM rose 21.7% YoY to 56 days metro-wide; in 65804, homes now average ~31 days on market vs 21 a year ago.
- List-to-sale ratio ~99%; typical homes sell slightly below list, while hot homes still go ~2% above list.
- Net read: demand is healthy but no longer frantic — sellers still hold the leverage, buyers are regaining negotiating room.

---

## 3. New Construction

### Building Activity
- The Home Builders Association of Greater Springfield tracks monthly permits for Springfield/Greene County; permit activity continues at a steady (not boom-level) pace. City permit fee schedules were updated July 2025 and the permitting process is being revamped effective May 2026 — a sign of sustained development volume.
- Most new single-family growth is concentrated in suburban Christian County (Nixa, Ozark) rather than within 65804, which is largely built out. Infill and teardown-rebuild activity dominates inside the ZIP.

### New vs Existing
- New construction in the metro prices well above the existing-home median, widening the value gap that keeps resale demand strong in established southeast Springfield neighborhoods.
- No evidence of overbuilding: supply additions are tracking demand, limiting downside risk.

### Rental Fundamentals
- **Vacancy rate: ~5.9%** — healthy, neither tight nor soft.
- Citywide gross rental yield: ~$13,350 annual rent ÷ ~$225K avg value ≈ **5.9% gross yield** — solid for a stable Midwest market. In 65804, higher purchase prices (~$296K median) compress yields to roughly 5.0–5.5% unless targeting 3BR+ product ($1,507+/mo).
- Demand anchors: Missouri State University (~25K students), two major hospital systems, and workforce housing demand. 2026 rent growth projected at 2–4%.

### Short-Term Rentals
- Springfield STRs average **46–58% occupancy and $100–131 ADR** depending on data source — modest economics versus vacation markets.
- Regulation is permissive but formal: STRs (<30 days) require city registration/licensing ($30 inspection fee + $25–105/yr business license based on receipts), zoning occupancy limits apply, and sales + transient guest taxes must be remitted. No ban; hosted and un-hosted both allowed with license.

- Employment grew ~3.0% in the most recent measured year. Metro GDP is **$29.3 billion**, up 73% over the past decade.
- Dominant industries: healthcare & social assistance (largest), retail trade, accommodation/food service, transportation/logistics, education.

### Population & Migration

- City population: ~170,000. Growth is steady inbound migration from rural Missouri/Arkansas and cost-of-living migrants from coastal and larger Midwest metros — not boom-driven, which makes it durable.

## 6. Infrastructure & Development

### Active Projects
- **I-44 "Forward 44" reconstruction** — rebuilding and widening I-44 from 4 to 6 lanes between US 65 and Kansas Expressway, with overpass replacements; current phase completing ~2027. Major long-term connectivity upgrade.
- **Capital Improvement Program: 130 projects, ~$452M planned 2026–2030** across streets, stormwater, and sewer.
- **Pediatric healthcare expansion** — both CoxHealth and Mercy have announced new pediatric medical care centers.
- **Springfield Public Schools construction** — new Pipkin Middle School and Reed Academy.
- **Highway MM corridor** expansion on the metro's west side.

### Future Catalysts
- Continued healthcare-sector capital investment benefits 65804 directly (the ZIP sits near the Mercy campus and major medical corridors).
- Citizens' Advisory Board recommended ~$26M in additional FY2027 infrastructure investments.

## 8. Risk Factors

1. **Affordability ceiling — MEDIUM.** Citywide price-to-income (~4.6x) is elevated for local wages (~$46K median household income). 65804's +7.7% YoY pace is unlikely to be sustainable without income growth.
2. **Interest rate sensitivity — MEDIUM.** Entry-level-heavy market; a 1% rate rise materially shrinks the qualified buyer pool, though low absolute prices cushion the payment impact versus coastal metros.
3. **Economic concentration — MEDIUM.** Healthcare (CoxHealth + Mercy = 22,000+ jobs) dominates. Offset by HQ presence of Bass Pro, O'Reilly, Prime Inc. and the university.
4. **Overbuilding risk — LOW.** Permits track demand; no oversupply signal. Most new supply lands in suburban Christian County.
5. **Migration reversal — LOW.** Growth is cost-of-living and regional-draw driven, not speculative; metro just crossed 500K.
6. **Regulatory risk — LOW.** No rent control; STR rules are permissive with cheap licensing; Missouri is landlord-friendly.
7. **Natural disaster exposure — MEDIUM.** Tornado alley — severe storm and tornado risk is real (insurance premiums rising regionally); no hurricane/wildfire/earthquake exposure of note.
8. **Political/tax risk — LOW.** Missouri property taxes are moderate; no adverse changes pending.
9. **Seasonal volatility — LOW-MEDIUM.** Standard Midwest seasonality (spring surge, winter slowdown); February data reflects a seasonal trough.
10. **National headwinds — MEDIUM.** A national credit tightening or recession would slow sales, but Springfield's low price point historically makes it defensive in downturns.

## 9. Market Forecast (12-Month Outlook)

*All forecasts are projections, not certainties.*

### Price Outlook
Metro prices likely rise **+2% to +4%** over the next 12 months. ZIP 65804's +7.7% YoY pace should cool toward the metro average as DOM lengthens, but the southeast side's desirability supports continued outperformance of 1–2 points over the citywide figure.

### Inventory Outlook
Months of supply likely drifts from ~2.4 toward **3.0–3.5** as DOM extends and new listings normalize — still seller-favorable but the most balanced conditions since 2019.

### Demand Outlook
Steady. Population crossing 500K, 4.1% unemployment, hospital expansion, and university demand underpin transaction volume; February's +10.8% YoY sales growth suggests buyers re-engaged after the 2024–25 rate plateau.

### Rate Impact
- Rates fall ~1%: pent-up entry-level demand releases; expect a quick return to sub-2-month supply and 5%+ appreciation.
- Rates flat: base case above.
- Rates rise ~1%: sales volume drops, DOM stretches past 60 days metro-wide, prices flatten (0% to +2%) but are unlikely to decline given supply tightness.

---

## 10. Bottom Line

Springfield, MO — and ZIP 65804 specifically — is a **stable, affordable, seller-leaning market in late expansion**, with healthcare-anchored employment, a metro that just crossed 500,000 residents, and a major I-44 rebuild underway. The best-fit strategy is **buy-and-hold (or BRRRR)** targeting the metro's ~5–6% gross yields and steady 2–4% appreciation; flips and STRs work only with disciplined purchase pricing. The biggest risks are the local affordability ceiling (modest incomes vs a 65804 median near $296K) and lengthening days on market signaling the post-2021 froth is fully gone.
